Dakini Victim Complex

Definition: A pattern where the author equates critique of her ideas or behavior with an attack on all women, or the sacred feminine principle.

Usage:

  • “This is how awakened women are treated in Tibetan Buddhism.”
  • “They fear the truth of the feminine voice.”

Purpose:

  • Protects herself from specific critique by generalizing it.
  • Transforms disagreement into oppression.
  • Gains moral sympathy while avoiding direct accountability.

Factional Framing

Definition: A narrative technique that casts relationships between respected Buddhist leaders or lineages in terms of rivalry, conflict, or ideological opposition — often by emphasizing selective quotes, events, or interpretations.

Usage:

  • “Why should anyone praise the Dalai Lama and Gelugpa in such nyperbolic absurd way? This has to be satire as people must resent their dominance over Tibet for too long.”
  • “Thaye Dorje is never anyone, only Ogyen Trinley is the real Karmapa.”

Purpose:

  • Positions the author as an insider revealing hidden disputes.
  • Encourages readers to see harmony as unlikely or impossible.
  • Amplifies the perception of instability within the tradition, increasing the author’s perceived importance as interpreter.

Leader Pitting

Definition: The deliberate juxtaposition of statements, teachings, or symbolic gestures from two respected leaders to imply direct opposition — often by omitting context or nuance.

Usage:

  • “The Karmapa has mentioned the past Mongol-Gelug issue that the Dalai Lama still refuses to address.”
  • “Unlike the Dalai Lama, the Karmapa speaks the real truth about vegetarian issue.”

Purpose:

  • Encourages readers to take sides between leaders who may not actually be in conflict.
  • Elevates the author’s role as mediator, truth-bearer, or faction champion.
  • Uses the prestige of one figure to diminish the credibility of another.
